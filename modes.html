Modes of the Major Scale

There are 7 modes in a Major scale, one for each note. An easy way to think about modes is to imagine starting a scale, ex. C Major, on a note other than its root. If you start C on its 3rd note and play, E F G A B C D E, you will have just played the E phrygian mode.

These are called relative modes because they are relative to a music key, in this case C Major.

There are also Parallel Modes. Parallel Modes do not share the same Key. Instead they share the same starting note. An example would be C Major and C Lydian. Each start on the note, C, but belong to different keys.
